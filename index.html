Overall Introduction

A bank wants to understand their customer base to see what type of customers would be willing to subscribe to a term deposit. We want to know the customers' demographics to identify which age group, profession, and educational background typically would subscribe, so that the bank will know who to target.

This topic is compelling as it demonstrates how predictive analytics bridges a bank's need for stable capital and a customer's pursuit of long-term financial security. Ultimately, this project will reveal the underlying patterns of how different segments of the population prioritize savings, allowing the bank to optimize resources and better connect with consumers.

Introduction to the Data

The data used in this research were sourced from Kaggle and consist of 100,000 observations related to banking customers. In terms of the variables used in the data, there are 45 of them, among which personal traits like age, gender, occupation, annual income, and level of education; financial metrics, such as net worth and investment portfolio value; campaign variables like response score, marketing score, and the number of contacts. Among all these variables, the most important one is called TermDepositSubscribed, which indicates if the customer subscribed to the term deposit or not.

In terms of the data type, it includes both categorical variables like job title, educational level, and type of contacts, as well as continuous variables like annual income and response propensity, which makes the data appropriate for multivariate analysis. Moreover, for better visualizations, some additional variables were created by the researchers themselves, including wealth class and age range.

Subscription Overview, Campaign Behavior & Wealth Segmentation

In total, 30% of customers opted for the term deposit offer, suggesting satisfactory success rates for the marketing campaign but a high level of room for improvement. About 70% were unconverted, which may imply that existing targeting efforts may not have targeted the relevant customers. The analysis of marketing campaign behavior suggests that higher call response rates are strongly linked to the chance of converting customers, with the most evident clustering taking place in the middle ranges of response rates, thereby implying a large share of unexploited opportunities. In terms of wealth levels, it is obvious that conversion rates progressively improve from low to high wealth classes.

Subscription Rate by Age Group and Job Title

The heatmap reveals which age and profession combinations are most likely to subscribe. Darker cells indicate higher subscription rates. Hover over any cell to see the exact percentage.

The heatmap shows different patterns regarding age and occupation variables. According to the information presented, the darkest colors in the table refer to management and entrepreneur occupation classes throughout all age groups, whose subscription rates vary from 38% to 46% — higher than the average level of 30%. The self-employed occupation category also appears to be above average for most age groups. On the contrary, students, retired, and blue-collar workers have comparatively lighter colors regardless of their age, hence proving that occupation is a better determinant of a client's performance than age. The highest subscription rate appears in the 30s–40s age group combined with management occupation class at approximately 43–44%, making mid-aged professionals the most profitable customer segment.

Marketing Strategy Recommendation

The bubble chart identifies the most actionable customer segments for the next campaign, combining marketing score, response propensity, and wealth profile into a single view.

The bubble plot considers various factors, such as marketing score, response rate, and wealth level, and provides an effective visualization technique. The ideal target customers belong to the top-right quadrant where they have high scores in the marketing category and low scores in the risk factor, while their response rates are relatively high (38–46%), and they include Management and Entrepreneur types who belong to Premium/Private customer groups. Such customers have high responsiveness and adequate financial ability. There is another group of customers who have good potential, but they belong to the Technician, Admin, and Self-employed types with above-average response rates (31–32%). Finally, students, blue-collar workers, and retired people are mostly located in the bottom-left quadrant since their response rates are relatively low along with poor financial readiness and engagement, which makes it an unattractive target group.

Campaign Path: Which Contact Method Led to Subscriptions?

The Sankey diagram traces the full campaign flow: from customer segment to contact channel to final outcome. Hover over any flow or node to see exact counts and percentages.

The Sankey diagram maps out the entire process of customer conversion from the segment to the channel of contact through to the final step of subscribing. Cellular marketing forms the largest percentage of contact and generates a higher conversion rate compared to telephone, hence is the dominant channel. The Mass and Standard segments form the majority of customer traffic but have low conversion rates, while the Premium and Private segments, which form a minority, have higher conversion rates despite the mode of contact used. This shows that the customer segment and wealth profile have more weight in determining conversion than the channel itself. Though telephone plays an important role in accessing specific segments, it does not need to be the major investment area. In general, the diagram suggests that focusing on high-wealth, high-conversion rate segments via cellular outreach will be most effective.

Summary and Additional Work

In conclusion, only 30% of the 100,000 customers analyzed chose to join in a long-term deposit, suggesting an adequate level of success but with much room left for optimization. The subscription decision is based on three major determinants:

  • Financial capacity — conversion rate grows steadily from 20.5% in the lowest earning people to 47.0% in the wealthiest ones;
  • Engagement quality — a direct connection is noted between better call response scores and increased chances to subscribe;
  • Demographic profile — the most common age group among users is 30–50 years. Managers and entrepreneurs have the best conversion rate at 38–46%, while students, blue-collar workers, and retired persons have lower conversion rates than 30%.

To improve future campaigns, four strategies are recommended:

  • Focus on high value clients — direct marketing efforts at customers who are managers or entrepreneurs in the medium to upper classes, between ages 30-50.
  • Expand into scalable segments — people in technician, administrative, and self-employment categories have above average conversion rates (about 31-32%) and form a substantial, unexplored base.
  • Optimize contact strategy — cell phones should be made the dominant method, and high propensity customers targeted through this means.
  • Reduce low-return targeting — students, working class people, and retirees are not the best prospects for direct marketing efforts.
